Singapore's Expansion causes environmental degradation and species extinction
With nature reserves sacrificed for resources, Singapore's rapid expansion has sparked global debate and controversy over environmental sacrifice
Benedict Chua
SUTD Undergraduate
Updated Nov 08, 2050, 07:01 PM
In a move that has captured global attention, Singapore has doubled in size over just a few short years—an achievement hailed as remarkable but not without controversy. As the city-state expands its footprint through innovative floating platforms and extensive new developments, questions have arisen regarding the resources fuelling this unprecedented growth. Recently released data reveals the answer, and it is both surprising and contentious.

With natural resources becoming increasingly scarce in the year 2050 and prices for imports soaring, Singapore faced a difficult choice. Unable to afford the high cost of imported materials, the government turned to large-scale mining and resource harvesting on its own nature reserves and surrounding islands.

These areas, once dedicated to conservation, housed a rich diversity of plants, animals, and other species that are now under severe threat. Destruction of these habitats has led to habitat loss, pushing species to extinction and undoing years of conservation work. Reports estimate that at least six species have gone extinct as a result, causing large-scale ecosystem destabilization and a significant loss of biodiversity.

Government officials have defended the decision as a necessary response to overpopulation. As the city’s population continued to soar, land for housing and infrastructure grew critically limited, and drastic measures became inevitable. The plan to use local resources was put to a public vote, with the majority supporting it due to overcrowding and increasing discomfort in daily life.

“It’s crazy eh, whenever I try to drive home from work in CBD (Central Business District) during peak hour, I would be stuck in a traffic jam for more than an hour. I think at this point you might as well not own a car anymore. Just harvest the materials from the nature reserves and expand Singapore already, who cares if some species goes extinct, they are not important anyway” said one resident from Bedok, who chose to remain anonymous.

However, despite the majority supporting the plan, a vocal minority opposed it, going so far as to organize protests or relocate to another country to distance themselves from the cause. The situation has sparked a global debate on whether this was an appropriate trade-off to secure a higher quality of life in Singapore, or an irreversible loss to natural heritage? As nations observe Singapore’s bold land expansion, they confront a larger ethical question: how much is Singapore willing to sacrifice for growth and comfort?

Singapore’s hawker culture to close by 2030 as new employment system reshapes workforce
A declining interest among younger generations and the demands of the trade have led the government to phase out hawker centers
Benedict Chua
SUTD Undergraduate
Updated Nov 08, 2050, 07:01 PM
SINGAPORE - Singapore’s beloved hawker culture, a staple of the nation’s identity since the 1800s and listed on UNESCO’s Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, is set to disappear within months as changing career paths and new employment systems reshape the workforce.

A recent survey of former hawkers reveals that many are leaving the trade due to old age, long working hours, low income, underappreciation, and the physically demanding nature of the job. And with the help from Singapore’s new education and employment system that harnesses AI technology to guarantee jobs through its dynamic job-matching algorithms, a significant number of hawkers have decided to seek alternative careers and have successfully trained and transitioned into less labour-intensive roles with higher pay.

Meanwhile, for younger generations, hawker careers seem to be increasingly unattractive. Data reveals that less than 2% of students would consider becoming hawkers and would rather opt for higher-paying blue- and white-collar professions. Therefore, in response to the resulting manpower shortage in hawker centres, the government has made the difficult decision to close all hawker centres by the end of 2030.

Moving forward, more food courts, restaurants, and standalone food outlets will be established to fill the void left by hawker centres. However, with this shift, potential price increases are expected as the food industry consolidates.

As such, the decision has sparked an online debate over the impact of the new system and the loss of Singapore’s hawker culture. Some argue that it has improved opportunities and income security, while others blame it for the cause of diminishing Singapore’s cultural heritage and national identity. As Singapore bids farewell to its hawker centres, the question lingers: is this a step forward or are we digging ourselves into a hole? And what other cherished traditions might fade away in the years to come?
